A model upgrade in the background does not mean much until you feel it in your actual work. This week, you will feel it. **OpenAI announced GPT-5.6**, and Microsoft is making it the preferred model inside Microsoft 365 Copilot across Word, Excel, PowerPoint, Copilot Chat, and the new Cowork experience. That means the same Copilot you already know now reasons a little deeper, drafts a little cleaner, and collaborates a little more naturally — without you needing to learn a new interface or move your files anywhere.

For most people, the headline is simple: Copilot got smarter. For anyone building workflows, automations, or agents on top of Microsoft 365, the headline is more interesting. The model powering the platform just leveled up, and that upgrade ripples into every agent, skill, and integration that calls Copilot.

Here is what changed, where the improvements show up, and a practical plan for trying them before lunch.

## What GPT-5.6 Brings to Copilot

OpenAI describes GPT-5.6 as its latest flagship series, designed to deliver more useful work per token with stronger performance on complex tasks. Translated into Microsoft 365 terms, that means Copilot can handle longer context, tighter reasoning, and more ambitious cross-app tasks without as much hand-holding.

Microsoft and OpenAI highlighted four specific improvements inside the productivity apps:

- **In Word**, Copilot can draft, edit, and refine documents with fewer rounds of prompting. That first-pass draft should need less massaging before it matches your voice and intent.
- **In Excel**, Copilot can support deeper analysis while using tokens more efficiently, so you move faster from raw data to real insight.
- **In PowerPoint**, Copilot can turn early ideas into more polished, visually compelling presentations with less manual guidance.
- **In Cowork**, the new cross-functional collaboration experience, Copilot can help complete complex, multi-team work with less manual coordination.

The last one deserves extra attention. Cowork is designed for the kind of work that spills across meetings, documents, chats, and project plans. If Copilot can keep context across that surface, it becomes more of a teammate and less of a chatbot.

## Why This Matters for Microsoft 365 Agents

The model underneath Copilot is the same model that Microsoft 365 agents increasingly rely on. When GPT-5.6 becomes the preferred model, it is not only the chat pane that benefits. Any agent built on Microsoft 365 Copilot, any declarative agent in Copilot Studio, and any skill using the Microsoft 365 Copilot reasoning layer gets a boost.

That matters because the bar for useful agents keeps rising. Early agents impressed us with simple retrieval and summarization. The next generation of agents needs to reason across apps, maintain state over longer workflows, and produce outputs that are actually production-ready. A stronger model is one of the ingredients that makes that transition possible.

For developers, the practical implication is that agent skills, orchestration logic, and prompt engineering you built for earlier Copilot behavior may now perform better than expected. It is a good time to re-run those prompts, re-test those skills, and see which ones now clear the quality threshold without extra work.

## The Cowork Angle: Copilot as Cross-Functional Partner

Cowl is the newest member of the Microsoft 365 family, and it is designed for work that does not fit neatly into one app. A marketing launch spans Word briefs, Excel budgets, PowerPoint decks, Teams chats, Outlook emails, and Planner tasks. Cowork is the place where that work comes together, and Copilot is the assistant that can see the whole picture.

With GPT-5.6, Copilot in Cowork can do more of the heavy lifting across those boundaries. Instead of asking Copilot to draft an email, then asking again to update a plan, then asking again to create a slide, you can describe the larger outcome and let Copilot coordinate the pieces. It is a step toward the kind of agentic collaboration Microsoft has been talking about for years — where the assistant helps complete work, not just answer questions about it.

## Try It This Week: A Five-Step Copilot Test Plan

The best way to evaluate a model upgrade is to test it on real work you already have. Here is a low-risk plan that takes less than an hour:

1. **Open a stale Word document.** Pick a report, proposal, or summary that has been sitting in your drafts. Ask Copilot to rewrite it for a different audience or update it with a new framing. Compare the first draft to what you remember from previous Copilot outputs.
2. **Hand Copilot a messy Excel file.** Import a real dataset — sales, expenses, project metrics — and ask for three insights you might not have noticed. See if the analysis goes beyond surface-level observations.
3. **Start a PowerPoint from a sentence.** Give Copilot a one-sentence idea and a target audience. Let it build the outline and first few slides. Focus on whether the structure makes sense before you worry about the design.
4. **Run a cross-app task in Cowork.** If you have access to the Cowork preview, describe a small project — planning a team offsite, preparing a quarterly review, launching an internal announcement — and let Copilot coordinate the documents, tasks, and communications.
5. **Re-test an agent or skill.** If you have built a Copilot Studio agent or Microsoft 365 agent with Agent Skills, run a few of your hardest test cases again. Note which behaviors improved and which still need prompting help.

Keep a short note of what worked better and what still fell short. Model upgrades are not magic, but they are momentum. Knowing exactly where the momentum shows up helps you invest your time in the right places.

## Token Efficiency Is Quietly Important

One of the less visible improvements in GPT-5.6 is token efficiency. In plain terms, the model does more with the context it is given. That might sound like a backend detail, but it has real consequences:

- Longer documents and larger spreadsheets can be reasoned over without truncating as aggressively.
- Multi-turn conversations in Copilot Chat stay coherent longer before earlier turns are forgotten.
- Agents that chain several tool calls together spend fewer tokens reconstructing context between steps.

For organizations running Copilot at scale, better token efficiency can translate into lower cost and higher quality at the same time. For individual users, it translates into fewer moments where Copilot seems to lose the thread halfway through a task.

## A Word on the Microsoft-OpenAI Partnership

The fact that Microsoft is making GPT-5.6 the preferred model in Microsoft 365 Copilot is another reminder of how closely the two companies are aligned in the productivity layer. Microsoft is not just embedding a model. It is integrating it across the apps, identity system, compliance boundary, and agent framework that enterprises rely on.

For customers, that integration is the point. A standalone chat interface is useful. A model that understands your documents, meetings, calendar, and organizational context — and stays inside your existing governance boundary — is transformative. Microsoft 365 Copilot is built to be that integrated experience, and GPT-5.6 is the latest engine underneath it.

## What to Watch Next

Model upgrades tend to unlock features more than announce them. As GPT-5.6 rolls out across Microsoft 365, watch for:

- Agents and skills that now complete tasks end-to-end instead of stopping halfway.
- Copilot Studio prompts that need fewer examples to behave consistently.
- Excel and PowerPoint outputs that require less manual cleanup.
- Cowork scenarios where Copilot can genuinely coordinate work across people and apps.

The most interesting effects often show up a few weeks after launch, once teams have had time to test their real workflows. If you keep a short log of what you try, you will be ahead of most users when the new behaviors become obvious.

## Bottom Line

GPT-5.6 in Microsoft 365 Copilot is the kind of upgrade that rewards curiosity. The interface is the same, but the ceiling is higher. The best thing you can do this week is run a few real tasks through it and notice where the quality step-change appears.

Copilot is not becoming something new. It is becoming a better version of the colleague it already promised to be.
